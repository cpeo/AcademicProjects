The Evolution of Shyanne Sellers’ Path to Leadership

By Cassie Peo

A 4-year-old little girl, playing in a basketball game with 5-year-olds, rushes down the court and immediately dribbles behind her back - correctly. After the play, she turns to look at her dad on the sideline, sending him a smirk that says, “See, I can do it.”

The little girl was Shyanne Sellers. Just the day before, she saw some older kids dribble behind their back and wanted to try it. That's when her dad told her to stick to the basics, but even then, she was determined to make her game better. And that was when her dad knew she was on the right path.

Shyanne Sellers is a 6-foot-2 guard in her junior year with the University of Maryland women’s basketball team and has taken up a prominent leadership role following the departure of Diamond Miller and Abby Meyers, who have gone onto play for the WNBA.

This season, Shyanne is averaging 18.1 points, 6.1 rebounds and 5.0 assists per game. She is also averaging almost 32 minutes played per game and has nearly doubled her number of assists per game since the start of her collegiate basketball career.

Maryland is relying on Shyanne to become a more prominent scorer and team leader this season, but she isn’t worried or focused on replacing her former teammates.

“I don't think I feel any added pressure, you know, I'm still just going to play my game,” Shyanne said.

But how is this young, 20-year-old athlete able to brush off the pressure and step into a leadership role so easily? If you ask anyone from her hometown in Ohio who knew Shyanne, they will tell you she hasn’t even hit her peak yet.

“Maryland ain’t seen nothing yet,” Brad Sellers, Shyanne’s father, said.

Her passion for basketball started young.

“Shyanne started really liking basketball, probably as soon as she could walk,” said Kym Sellers, Shyanne’s mother. “She would have a ball in her hand at all times.”

That passion motivated Kym to get her daughter working with a coach early. Kym lied about Shyanne’s age so she could start playing at 4-years-old when she was supposed to be 5; Shyanne has been playing up ever since.

Shyanne was always talented, but you could tell that she was a born leader, according to her AAU basketball coach and mentor Carlos Shea-Ron. He recalled how, from day one with him, Shyanne would tell other players where to go in drills, or how to fix what they were doing wrong.

Her parents and mentor noted that, while others her age might think that they know everything, Shyanne recognized early on that there is always room to grow and help those around her. Kym said Shyanne has always made a point to take people under her wing and wants the best for her teammates.

“Carlos was the turning point. He really was able to get [Shyanne] and mold her and let her know that she was something special,” Kym said. “Carlos changed her whole mentality about the game and showed her that she can go further.”

During Shyanne’s first practice with Shea-Ron, she was playing with a fifth-grade team at the time while only being in third grade herself. This would not be unusual for Shyanne, though, as she played up in higher levels constantly with her three older sisters throughout her early career.

As a 12-year-old, Shea-Ron let Shyanne play with his high school team after her spring season ended and would take her to tournaments for further exposure.

“She had been going out on that big circuit since she was a baby, and trust me, she handled herself well,” he said.

In a tournament that same year, Shyanne was playing with her older sister’s team and caught the attention of other coaches and players.

“Just as I’m walking in, she's laying the ball up against two defenders. She was so young, but she was so good,” Shea-Ron said.

By her freshman year in high school, Shyanne had grown into herself as a player and was confident in her ball-handling and leadership abilities. In a game her freshman year, Shyanne’s older sister Shayla was unable to play and everyone in the stands was worried because this would be the first time Shyanne would play a high school game without her sister.

“Shyanne finally looked up at everybody and said, ‘I got this. Just relax, I got this,’” Kym said.

Shyanne stepped onto the court confident and collected, and went on to make five 3-point shots. Unfortunately, the team lost at the buzzer, but Kym said this was the first time Shyanne stepped up as a leader for her high school team.

Before she got to Maryland, Shyanne focused her play on offense. But when she arrived in College Park, she learned if she was going to play at this level, she needed to play defense, too.

Brad remembers Shyanne saying, “I am going to play myself into the lineup.”

Throughout her freshman year, Shyanne worked hard on her defensive game and averaged 3.8 rebounds, 1.8 steals and 0.2 blocks; she also averaged 7.7 points and 2.6 assists per game. As a result, she was named to the All-Big Ten Freshman team.

Even during her time as a freshman, Shyanne felt like she could be a leader for the team as she was able to capitalize on the examples around her, like Miller and Meyers, and her coaches, and learn from them.

Assistant coach Lindsey Spann spoke to how Shyanne has grown as a player over the course of her college career, saying Shyanne has always been constantly asking questions and is now able to relay messages to her teammates, both in-game and at practices.

“It's really nice to see where she has grown in her mentality,” Spann said.

Shyanne said she has taken freshman Riley Nelson under her wing this season because she sees her younger self in Nelson. Her goal, Shyanne said, is to help the freshmen transition onto the team better and put them in a good headspace because she recognizes how important freshman players can be, especially for Maryland’s deep bench this season.

When asked what her strongest attribute was, Shyanne said it was her ability to see the floor and problem solve.

“She's actually seeing the game and talking her teammates through scenarios,” Spann said. “That's one of the beautiful things about coaching, it’s literally watching your players develop and mature.”

Ever since she was little, Shyanne has always prioritized her team and teammates, according to her parents. Kym said Shyanne loves to be able to facilitate, that she likes pushing the ball off to her teammates and likes being able to be a playmaker. Shyanne averaged 2.6 assists per game her freshman year, 3.9 her sophomore year and 5.0 per game so far this season.

Shaynne said her goals this season are to hold herself to the highest standard and make sure her team does well.

She has evolved as a player during her time at Maryland and has taken on a prominent leadership role this season, but, as noted by her parents and everyone around her, Shyanne was always born to lead.

Her father said he knows Shyanne is growing into her role as a leader even more this season because he doesn’t get as many calls asking him what to do or how to solve a problem. He says her problem-solving skills, both on and off the court, have excelled and he is confident in her ability to lead the Maryland Women’s Basketball team to success.

“She put [herself] in a really, really good position to be successful here for us and to lead the team,” said Spann.

Shyanne has been asking for more responsibility on the team since her freshman year at Maryland but said there is a difference between asking and when you know you are ready.

“I think, this year, I've just taken a lot of accountability on myself, my actions and doing the right things to lead this team,” Shyanne said. “In the past two seasons, I've just been young and trying to learn as much as possible, especially from people that are older than me. So, I think this year is the year that I am truly ready.”
